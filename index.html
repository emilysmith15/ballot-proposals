New Yorkers will vote on six ballot proposals this election season.

Many of them could shift power structures in New York’s City Charter.

By Emily Smith

When New Yorkers go to the polls on Nov. 4, they will be met with six ballot proposals, three of which would change power structures concerning New York City’s highly consequential Uniform Land Use Review Procedure (ULURP).

  1. Amendment to Allow Olympic Sports Complex in Essex County on State Forest Preserve Land

    2. A "yes" vote would allow for expanded skiing and related trail facilities on a site of 1,039 acres in the Adirondack forest preserve.

    The Adirondack Park hosts the Olympic Sports Complex, where this proposal aims to expand trail facilities while compensating for the lost public land by adding 2,500 acres of new protected land.

  2. Fast Track Affordable Housing to Build More Affordable Housing Across the City

    3. A "yes" vote would allow for the creation of two new processes for fast tracking certain affordable housing projects.

    The first option would be available to Housing Development Fund Companies (HDFCs) – responsible for practically all publicly financed 100% affordable housing projects in NYC – and would allow the Board of Standards and Appeals (BSA) to approve projects review from the local Community Board, who could offer recommendations.

    The second option would create “a new public review procedure for applications that deliver affordable housing in the community districts that permit the least affordable housing” under the City’s Mandatory Inclusionary Housing (MIH) program. The City Planning Commission (CPC) would make the final decision after recommendations from the local Community Board.

  3. Simplify Review of Modest Housing and Infrastructure Projects

    4. A "yes" vote simplifies review for limited land-use changes, including modest housing and minor infrastructure projects.

    This proposal would create an Expedited Land Use Review Procedure (ELURP) for projects that “categorically lack potential significant adverse impacts on communities.” The final decision for these projects would lie with the CPC.

  4. Establish an Affordable Housing Appeals Board with Council, Borough, and Citywide Voices

    5. A "yes" vote would create a three-member Affordable Housing Appeals Board made up of the local Borough President, Speaker of the City Council and Mayor which could reverse City Council decisions about affordable housing projects.

  5. Create a Digital City Map to Modernize City Operations

    6. A "yes" vote would create a consolidated, digital City Map.

    The Department of City Planning would be responsible for creating, maintaining, and digitizing a single map, instead of the current system in which five topographical bureaus across the Borough Presidents’ offices are responsible.

  6. Move Local Elections to Presidential Election Years to Increase Voter Participation

    7. A "yes" vote would move city elections to the same years as federal presidential elections, when permitted by state law.

    Current city elections happen on odd numbered years while federal presidential elections are on even numbered years. This would affect the city offices of mayor, public advocate, comptroller, borough president, and city council.
